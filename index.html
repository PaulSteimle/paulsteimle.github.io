About Me

I am currently a research assistant at the Max Planck Institute for Astronomy, continuing the work of my Master's Thesis. My research specializes mainly in the detection and characterization of planets and brown dwarfs around nearby stars. I utilize high-contrast imaging techniques and work with ground-based telescopes such as the VLT and space-based telescopes such as JWST.

Previously, I obtained my master's degree with excellence in physics with a specialization in astronomy from the Ruprecht-Karls-University in Heidelberg in 2025. I spent half of my master's degree during the academic year 23/24 at the University of New Mexico in Albuquerque, where I attended lectures on astronomy and performed research on the development of a single transit event detection pipeline for TESS. I obtained my Bachelor's degree from Ruprecht-Karls-University in Heidelberg in 2023, with a thesis at the State Observatory Heidelberg, where I re-analyzed a radial velocity survey of giant stars.

Research

Debris disks are circumstellar dust disks that are thought to be analogs of the Kuiper Belt in our own Solar System. They consist of dust and planetesimals produced by ongoing collisions of larger bodies and trace the architecture of the outer regions of planetary systems. Because debris disks are shaped by gravitational interactions with planets, they provide valuable clues about the presence and properties of otherwise unseen companions and about the processes that govern planet formation and evolution.
High-contrast imaging (HCI) is a powerful technique for studying these systems, as it allows the direct detection of faint planets and substellar companions at large orbital separationsâ€”regions that are inaccessible to most indirect exoplanet detection methods. Since the first direct image of an exoplanet by Marois et al. (2008) , HCI has advanced rapidly. Although more than 280 exoplanets and brown dwarfs have now been discovered with this technique, the population of wide-orbit planets and their role in shaping circumstellar disks remains poorly constrained.

Master Thesis

My research focuses on searching for giant planets in debris and protoplanetary disk systems to investigate whether and how such planets perturb their surrounding disks. To detect faint planetary and brown dwarf signals, I apply state-of-the-art data reduction and analysis techniques to imaging and spectroscopic observations obtained with VLT/SPHERE and the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST).

In earlier research projects, I worked on exoplanet reseach using the transit and radial velocity method. I improved a single transit search pipeline for TESS lightcurves during my time at the University of New Mexico. During my bachelor's thesis, I reanalyzed the Lick RV Survey for K and G giant stars using a new Python3 reduction pipeline for iodine spectra pyodine (Heeren et al. 2023).

TESS Single Transit Search

My work during my time at the University of New Mexico was mainly focussed on improving the single transit search pipeline for TESS lightcurves. Besides restructuring and organizing the code I implemeted a machine learning approach based on the python package Deep Transit (Cui et al. 2021).

Bachelor Thesis

My bachelor thesis deals with the reanalysis of the Lick RV Survey for K and G giant stars (Frink et al. 2001) using the new python3 reduction pipeline for iodine spectra pyodine (Heeren et al. 2023).
