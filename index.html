About Me

I am a Ph.D. candidate at the Institute for Astrophysics and Geophysics at the University of Göttingen, where my research focuses on the radial velocity method for detecting and characterizing exoplanets. My work centers on developing techniques to extract precise radial velocities from high-resolution, high-precision spectra (see Research).

Previously, I worked as a research assistant at the Max Planck Institute for Astronomy in Heidelberg, continuing the work I began during my Master's thesis on detecting and characterizing planets and brown dwarfs around nearby stars through high-contrast imaging with the Very Large Telescope in Chile and the James Webb Space Telescope.

I completed my master's degree with distinction in physics, specializing in astronomy, at Ruprecht-Karls-University Heidelberg in 2025. During the 2023/24 academic year, I studied as an exchange student abroad at the University of New Mexico in Albuquerque, where I took courses in astronomy and conducted research on developing a single-transit-event detection pipeline for TESS (see here). I earned my Bachelor's degree from Ruprecht-Karls-University Heidelberg in 2023, with a thesis completed at the State Observatory Heidelberg, where I re-analyzed a radial velocity survey of giant stars.

Besides my fascination for physics, I am passionate about cycling, football, coffee, photography and cooking, especially Italian food. If you ask me, I'd say it's super easy to make homemade pizza!

Research

Doctoral Studies

My doctoral research at the Institute for Astrophysics and Geophysics at the University of Göttingen focuses on the radial velocity (RV) method for exoplanet detection and characterization. It's one of the most powerful tools we have for studying planets around other stars. For one, it's the only way to actually determine the mass of a planet. Combinign this with the planet radius, measured from its transit depth, lets us determine the planet's density and composition. From this we can draw first conclusions about the planet's interior structure and figure out whether it's rocky like Earth, or Mars or more of a gas giant like Jupiter. RV data also lets us do something called a Rossiter-McLaughlin measurement, which tells us whether a planet orbits in the same direction its star spins, or at a tilt. Latter can hint towards a non-regular formation and evolution history of the planetary system.

While RV is one of the most productive techniques in the field, its ultimate precision is set by how well we can model the spectrograph itself including the instrument optics, detector response, and subtle systematics imprinted on every observed spectrum. Current state-of-the-art spectrographs can reach a precision of around 30 cm/s, which is enough to detect rocky planets around Sun-like stars. However a central goal is to push this precision even further, to the level of a few cm/s, which would allow us to detect Earth-like planets in the habitable zone of Sun-like stars. Translating this to the actual shift of the spectrum on the detector, this corresponds to a shift of only a fraction of a nanometer, which is about the width of a single atom.

To ultimate achieve this level of precision, we need to understand the spectrograph and its systematics at an unprecedented level. One method to obtain this understanding is to develop a forward-modelling technique that reconstructs observed spectra at the detector level, pixel by pixel. By folding the spectrograph's characteristics directly into the analysis, this approach aims to reduce systematic uncertainties on derived radial velocities and push towards higher precision.

Earlier Research

Master's Thesis — High Contrast Imaging of Giant Planets

Prior to my PhD, I worked at the Max Planck Institute for Astronomy on the search for giant planets in debris and protoplanetary disk systems using high-contrast imaging. Extra solar Kuiper belt analogs, so called debris disks, are the leftovers of planet formation and can be used to trace the architecture of planetary systems. Through planet-disk interactions, planets can sculpt the disk structure and create features such as gaps, rings, and warps. By studying these features we can infer the presence of planets and constrain their properties. However, directly detecting the planets responsible for these features is crucial to confirm their existence and to further constrain their properties. To detect these faint signals from planets and brown dwarfs, I applied state-of-the-art data reduction and analysis techniques to imaging and spectroscopic observations obtained with SPHERE Instrument at the Unit Telescope 3 of the Very Large Telescope (VLT) and the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST). In the context of this work I developed the data reduction pipeline REACHER, which applies all necessary corrections to turn raw telescope data into science-ready products. The pipeline is publicly available on GitHub.

Two papers from this work are currently in preparation (P. V. Steimle et al. and E. C. Matthews, P. V. Steimle et al.).

TESS Single Transit Search

Most planets discovered by the transit method reside in close proximity to their host stars and are found through multiple transits. These occur at regular intervals ranging from hours to tens of days, allowing for straightforward detection combined with a precise determination of the planet's orbital period. Comparing these periods to the solar system, the majority of planets in our own backyard are located further from the Sun and have orbital periods of years. Jupiter, for example, has an orbital period of 12 years, while the outer planets Uranus and Neptune have orbital periods of decades. Therefore, detecting these planets is more challenging. Capturing a single transit requires surveys with long-term monitoring of the host star, or relies on serendipitous detections in short-term surveys. In the latter case, without a priori knowledge of the event and the repetition of transits, these so-called single transit events are challenging to detect and confirm, as there is no direct way to determine their orbital period or rule out false positives.

My work at the University of New Mexico focused on improving the single transit search pipeline for TESS lightcurves. In addition to restructuring and organizing the existing codebase, I implemented a machine learning approach for transit event detection based on the Python package Deep Transit (Cui et al. 2021), which reduces the number of false positives and increases the efficiency of single transit event detection.

Bachelor Thesis

Investigating exoplanets around evolved stars is a unique opportunity to study planetary systems in a state further along in their evolution than our own Solar System. As stars age and evolve off the main sequence, they undergo significant changes in size, luminosity, and mass loss. These changes can have profound effects on the orbits and atmospheres of surrounding planets, providing a natural laboratory for understanding the long-term dynamics of planetary systems. By studying exoplanets around evolved stars, we can gain insights into the future of our own Solar System and the potential fate of its planets as the Sun ages. Additioanly, these evoled stars allow us to study planetary systems around stars that are more massive than the Sun, which are difficult to characterize precisely during their main sequence phase but become more tractable as they evolve into cooler, slower-rotating giants.

One survey that has been instrumental in this field is the Lick Radial Velocity Survey for K and G giant stars (Frink et al. 2001), which has provided a wealth of data on the radial velocities of these evolved stars. The Waltz spectrograph, located at a 0.7-meter telescope at the Landessternwarte Heidelberg, is bulit as a successor to the Lick survey. To analyze the data from this spectrograph, Heeren et al. ( 2023).

developed a new reduction pipeline for iodine spectra called pyodine, which is implemented in Python 3. My bachelor thesis focused on reanalyzing the archival Lick RV Survey data using this new pipeline, with the goal of improving the precision of radial velocity measurements and enhancing our ability to detect exoplanets around these evolved stars. The main goal of this work was to develop a method to provide exact wavelength calibrations based on iodine flat field images for the archival Lick RV Survey data, a key requirement for the successful application of the pyodine pipeline.
