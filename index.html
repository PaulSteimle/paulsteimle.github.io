About Me

I am a PhD candidate at the Institute for Astrophysics and Geophysics at the University of Göttingen, where my research focuses on the radial velocity method — combining general exoplanet science with the development of new techniques for analyzing high-precision, high-resolution spectra (see Research).

Prior to my PhD, I was a research assistant at the Max Planck Institute for Astronomy in Heidelberg, continuing the work of my Master's Thesis on the detection and characterization of planets and brown dwarfs around nearby stars using high-contrast imaging with the Very Large Telescope in Chile and the James Webb Space Telescope.

I obtained my master's degree with excellence in physics with a specialization in astronomy from the Ruprecht-Karls-University in Heidelberg in 2025. I spent half of my master's degree during the academic year 23/24 at the University of New Mexico in Albuquerque, where I attended lectures on astronomy and performed research on the development of a single transit event detection pipeline for TESS. I obtained my Bachelor's degree from Ruprecht-Karls-University in Heidelberg in 2023, with a thesis at the State Observatory Heidelberg, where I re-analyzed a radial velocity survey of giant stars.

Besides my fascination for physics, I am passionate about cycling, football, coffee, photography and cooking, especially Italian food. If you ask me, I'd say it's super easy to make homemade pizza!

Research

My doctoral research at the Institute for Astrophysics and Geophysics at the University of Göttingen focuses on the radial velocity (RV) method for exoplanet detection and characterization. While RV is one of the most productive techniques in the field, its ultimate precision is set by how well we can model the spectrograph itself — the instrument optics, detector response, and subtle systematics imprinted on every observed spectrum.

A central goal of my PhD is to develop a forward-modelling technique that reconstructs observed spectra at the detector level, pixel by pixel. By folding the spectrograph's characteristics directly into the analysis, this approach aims to reduce systematic uncertainties on derived radial velocities and push towards higher precision. Alongside this methodological work, I pursue broader exoplanet research using the radial velocity method.

Prior to my PhD, I worked at the Max Planck Institute for Astronomy on the search for giant planets in debris and protoplanetary disk systems using high-contrast imaging. Two papers from this work are currently in preparation (P. V. Steimle et al.; E. C. Matthews, P. V. Steimle et al.).

Master's Thesis & MPIA Research

This work focused on searching for giant planets in debris and protoplanetary disk systems to investigate whether and how such planets perturb their surrounding disks. To detect faint planetary and brown dwarf signals, I applied state-of-the-art data reduction and analysis techniques to imaging and spectroscopic observations obtained with SPHERE Instrument at the Unit Telescope 3 of the Very Large Telescope (VLT) and the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST). In the context of this work I developed the data reduction pipeline REACHER, which applies all necessary corrections to turn raw telescope data into science-ready products. The pipeline is publicly available on GitHub.

Earlier Research

TESS Single Transit Search

My work during my time at the University of New Mexico was mainly focused on improving the single transit search pipeline for TESS lightcurves. Besides restructuring and organizing the code I implemented a machine learning approach based on the python package Deep Transit (Cui et al. 2021).

Bachelor Thesis

My bachelor thesis deals with the reanalysis of the Lick RV Survey for K and G giant stars (Frink et al. 2001) using the new python3 reduction pipeline for iodine spectra pyodine (Heeren et al. 2023).
