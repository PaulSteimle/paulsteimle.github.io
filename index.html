Paul Steimle, M. Sc. Student

I'm an observational astronomer with key interest in exoplanets.

About Me

I'm current finishing my Master's degree in Physics and Astronomy at the Ruprecht-Karls-University in Heidelberg, with a specialization in the detection and characterization of planetary systems of nearby stars. My bachelor thesis at the State Observatory Heidelberg dealt with the re-analysis of a radial velocity survey of giant stars. I spent the academic year 23/24 at the University of New Mexico in Albuquerque, where I worked on a single transit event detection pipeline. As my master's thesis at the Max Planck Institute for Astronomy, I am conducting research on the discovery and characterization of exoplanets and debris disks using high-contrast imaging methods, both with ground-based and space-based telescopes. My main interest is the further development of post processing techniques that help us to better discover and understand distant worlds.

Besides my fascination for physics, I am passionate about photography and cooking, especially Italian food. If you ask me, I'd say it's super easy to make homemade pizza!

Research

My research is mainly related to exoplanets, more precisely to find them with the radial velocity or transit method.

Bachelor Thesis

My bachelor thesis deals with the reanalysis of the Lick Survey for K and G giant stars (Frink et al. 2001) using the new python3 reduction pipeline for iodine spectra pyodine (Heeren et al. 2023).

CTI Effect

During a research project at the State Observatory in Heidelberg I investigated the effect of the charge transfer inefficiency at the Waltz telescope

TESS Single Transit Search

My work during my time at the University of New Mexico was mainly focussed on improving the single transit search pipeline for TESS lightcurves. Besides restructuring and organizing the code I implemeted a machine learning approach based on the python package Deep Transit (Cui et al. 2021).
